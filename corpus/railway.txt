The mountain railway climbs nearly two kilometres in altitude over a route that engineers once declared impossible to build. To gain height within the narrow valley the line doubles back on itself through a series of spiral tunnels bored directly into the rock. Each spiral allows the train to cross over its own path at a higher level, trading horizontal distance for a gentle and manageable gradient. The construction took more than a decade and cost the lives of many labourers who worked through brutal alpine winters. When it opened, the railway cut a journey of several days to a matter of hours and opened the remote interior to trade and tourism. The original steam locomotives struggled with the thin mountain air, and the line was among the first to be converted to electric traction. Today the railway is prized as much for its scenery as for its utility, and special panoramic carriages carry visitors slowly past glaciers and high pastures. Maintenance remains a constant challenge, as rockfalls and avalanches threaten the exposed sections every year.
