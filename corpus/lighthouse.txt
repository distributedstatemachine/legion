The old lighthouse on the headland was automated in the late twentieth century, ending more than a hundred years of continuous human habitation. Before automation a keeper and his family lived in the attached cottage, tending the lamp through every night of the year regardless of weather. The light itself was driven by a clockwork mechanism that the keeper had to rewind every few hours, a duty that broke his sleep into short and exhausting fragments. Supplies arrived by boat once a month, and in winter storms the delivery was often delayed for weeks at a time. The lens, a great spiral of carefully ground glass, was imported at enormous expense and floated the beam far out across the shipping lanes. After automation the cottage fell into disrepair, but a local trust later restored it and opened it to visitors as a small museum. The original clockwork is still in place and is demonstrated to tourists on summer afternoons. The headland is now a protected site, valued both for its maritime heritage and for the seabirds that nest on the surrounding cliffs.
