The coral reef supports a density of life rivalled by few other places on the planet despite occupying a tiny fraction of the ocean floor. Corals are colonies of tiny animals that build limestone skeletons, and over thousands of years these skeletons accumulate into vast structures visible from orbit. The living coral depends on microscopic algae that live within its tissues, trading shelter for the sugars the algae produce through photosynthesis. When the water grows too warm the coral expels these algae and turns a ghostly white, an event known as bleaching that can kill the colony if it persists. Reefs shelter an enormous variety of fish, which in turn support fisheries that feed millions of people in coastal communities. They also protect shorelines from storm surges by absorbing the energy of incoming waves before it reaches the land. Scientists monitor reef health using underwater surveys and satellite measurements of sea surface temperature. Restoration projects now grow coral fragments in nurseries and transplant them onto degraded reefs, though the scale of the loss far outpaces these efforts.
