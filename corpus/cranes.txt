The migratory cranes follow the river valley each autumn and rest on the gravel banks before continuing south toward the estuary wetlands. Naturalists in the region have recorded their passage for over a century, noting that the birds arrive within the same two-week window almost every year. The cranes feed on waste grain left in the harvested fields, and farmers have learned to tolerate the brief visitation because the flocks move on quickly. At dusk the birds gather in shallow water where predators cannot approach unseen, and their calls carry for several kilometres across the flat country. Conservation groups maintain a small reserve along the main flyway, fencing off the most important roosting sites during the migration season. The reserve is funded partly by visitors who come to watch the spectacle, and the income has made the protection of the wetland economically self-sustaining. In years of drought the water level drops and the cranes are forced to seek alternative roosts, sometimes scattering across a much wider area. Researchers fit a small number of birds with lightweight tracking devices, and the resulting data has reshaped our understanding of the route and its critical stopover points.
