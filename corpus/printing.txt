The invention of movable type transformed the spread of knowledge across the continent within a single generation. Before printing, books were copied by hand in monastic scriptoria, a process so slow and costly that a single volume might represent years of labour. The new presses could produce hundreds of identical copies in the time a scribe needed for one, and the price of a book fell sharply as a result. This sudden abundance of text fuelled an explosion of literacy among the merchant and artisan classes, who had previously had little access to written works. Printers became influential figures in their cities, and the workshops they ran were among the first recognisably modern businesses, balancing capital, labour, and distribution. Authorities were quick to grasp the political power of the press and equally quick to attempt its control through licensing and censorship. Despite such efforts, printed pamphlets circulated widely and played a decisive role in the religious and intellectual upheavals of the period. The standardisation of texts also stabilised spelling and grammar, gradually shaping the modern form of several European languages.
