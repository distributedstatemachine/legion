A honeybee colony behaves in many respects like a single organism rather than a collection of individuals. The queen is the only reproductive female, and her output of eggs determines the size and vigour of the entire colony. Worker bees, all sterile females, divide their labour by age, progressing from cleaning and nursing duties to foraging in their final weeks of life. Foragers communicate the location of food through a precise waggle dance that encodes both the direction and distance of the source relative to the sun. The colony regulates the temperature of its brood nest with remarkable accuracy, clustering to generate heat in winter and fanning their wings to cool it in summer. When the colony grows crowded it raises a new queen and the old queen departs with a swarm to found a fresh nest. Beekeepers exploit these natural behaviours, providing hollow frames on which the bees build their comb and harvesting the surplus honey. The health of managed colonies has become a matter of wide concern, as pesticides, parasites, and disease interact to weaken them.
